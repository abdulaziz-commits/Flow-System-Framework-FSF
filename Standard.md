# Flow System Framework (FSF) — Standard v1.0

## 1. Purpose

The Flow System Framework (FSF) is an analytical standard for understanding and evaluating system behavior through the interaction between assets and flows within geographical constraints.

It provides a unified model for identifying bottlenecks, diagnosing instability, and guiding decision-making across complex systems.

---

## 2. Core Definition

A Flow System is defined as:

> The movement of flows within a geographical space, governed by mandatory assets and executed through operational assets, where system stability is determined at a node by comparing capacity and demand.

---

## 3. Core Model

The framework is centered on the following relationship:

SF = K / D

Where:

- K (Capacity): The maximum throughput generated by assets  
- D (Demand): The required or incoming flow  
- SF (Safety Factor): The ratio used to determine system stability  

---

## 4. Node Concept

The node is the central element of the framework.

> A node is the synchronization point of operational assets and flows within a geographical constraint, controlled by mandatory assets.

All system evaluation occurs at the node.

---

## 5. System Components

### 5.1 Mandatory Assets

Assets that regulate and enable the system:

- Human  
- Organizational  
- Technical  
- Geographical  

---

### 5.2 Operational Assets

Assets that execute the flow:

- Input Assets  
- Production Assets  
- Logistics Assets  

---

### 5.3 Flows

Elements that move within the system:

- Material  
- Human  
- Financial  
- Informational  

---

### 5.4 Geography

The system operates within a geographical constraint:

- Spatial (locations, facilities)  
- Transit (routes, corridors, channels)  

---

## 6. State Model

### 6.1 Node States (Mathematical)

Determined by SF:

- SF > 1 → Stable  
- SF = 1 → Critical  
- SF < 1 → Bottleneck  

---

### 6.2 Flow States (Operational)

Describes flow behavior:

- Stable  
- Unstable  
- Blocked  

---

### 6.3 Distinction

> Criticality is a mathematical state; instability is an operational flow condition.

---

## 7. Core Principle

> Assets generate capacity, flows generate demand, and the node determines the balance.

---

## 8. System Logic

> Operational assets synchronize, geography constrains, and mandatory assets control.

---

## 9. Bottleneck Definition

A bottleneck occurs when:

D > K

This condition may originate upstream but becomes measurable at the node.

---

## 10. Method of Use

To apply the FSF:

1. Identify the node  
2. Measure capacity (K)  
3. Identify demand (D)  
4. Compute SF = K / D  
5. Determine system state  

---

## 11. Applications

The FSF is applicable in:

- Logistics systems  
- Energy networks  
- Supply chains  
- Infrastructure planning  
- Strategic systems
